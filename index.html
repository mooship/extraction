The System
Is Rigged.

Wealth does not trickle down — it floods upward. The top 1% of the global population holds more combined wealth than the bottom 50%. This is not an accident. It is the designed outcome of a system built to extract value from workers and transfer it to capital owners. The data is unambiguous. The injustice is structural. The solution must be structural. And it must be collective.

Wealth Inequality

The Great
Upward
Transfer

Since the 1980s, the share of wealth held by the bottom half of the global population has collapsed. Wage suppression, financialisation, and tax policy have systematically redirected economic output from workers to shareholders across every major economy. In South Africa — the world's most unequal major economy — decades of neoliberal policy have entrenched apartheid-era wealth concentration under democratic governance. The middle class is not "squeezed" — it is being liquidated.

"To allow the market mechanism to be the sole director of the fate of human beings and their natural environment… would result in the demolition of society."
— Karl Polanyi, The Great Transformation, 1944

Source: World Inequality Database (Piketty, Saez, Zucman) · US Federal Reserve SCF · Oxfam Inequality Report 2025

Historical Record

A Century of
Concentrated Power

The post-war egalitarian era was not natural or inevitable — it was won through organised labour and political will. The neoliberal revolution, originating in the USA and exported globally through the IMF and World Bank, reversed those gains in a generation. The USA illustrates a structural pattern replicated across every economy that adopted the doctrine. The same neoliberal inflection point after 1980 appears in the UK, Germany, India, and South Africa — the USA is illustrative, not exceptional.

Source: World Inequality Database (Piketty, Saez, Zucman)

Tax Avoidance & Offshore Wealth

The Hidden
Trillions

The wealth concentration shown above is an undercount. An estimated $32+ trillion sits offshore — beyond democratic reach. Corporations shift over $1 trillion in profits to tax havens annually. The global average corporate statutory tax rate has been halved since 1985, from 49% to 23%, in a race to the bottom that makes redistribution structurally impossible. The UK's Crown Dependencies and Overseas Territories alone are responsible for 23% of global corporate tax losses.

Meanwhile, illicit financial flows strip $88 billion per year from Africa — more than the continent receives in aid. The system does not merely produce inequality — it actively shields it from democratic correction. Tax havens are not rogue actors. They are built into the architecture of global capital: the City of London, Delaware, the Netherlands.

"The tax system has been turned against the very people it is supposed to serve. When billionaires pay lower tax rates than their secretaries, it is not a loophole — it is the design."
— Gabriel Zucman, The Hidden Wealth of Nations, 2015

On the Race to the Bottom

States compete to attract capital by gutting their own tax bases. Ireland's 12.5% corporate rate drew multinationals away from neighbours. The Netherlands became a conduit for profit shifting on an industrial scale. Delaware offers corporate anonymity rivalling any Caribbean island. The result is a global architecture of tax competition that systematically defunds the public institutions needed to counteract inequality.

The 2021 global minimum tax agreement — set at just 15% — was hailed as historic. But it was designed with enough carve-outs to preserve the system it claimed to reform. A 15% floor does not end the race to the bottom — it merely sets a new basement. The democratic capacity to fund universal services, cancel debt, or finance a green transition is being dismantled one tax cut at a time.

Labour vs Capital

Workers Produce.
Capital Captures.

Since 1980, the share of national income going to workers has fallen in most major economies while the share captured by capital owners has soared. This is not productivity — it is extraction. The post-war social contract, forged through union power and democratic pressure, has been systematically dismantled through deregulation, union-busting, and offshoring to the Global South — where weaker labour protections and lower wages allow capital to extract even more — against the organised resistance of workers who face the same logic without the same protections.

"The share of income going to labour has declined in most economies since the 1980s, while returns to capital have risen correspondingly."
— ILO World Employment and Social Outlook, 2024
"The accumulation of capital at the centre is inseparable from the dispossession and impoverishment of the periphery."
— Samir Amin, Unequal Development, 1976

Source: BLS / BEA · Economic Policy Institute · ILO World Employment and Social Outlook 2024

Housing & Rent

Shelter as
Speculation

Housing has been transformed from a basic human need into a financial asset class. Homeownership rates are collapsing for younger cohorts across the Anglosphere while rents consume an ever-larger share of wages already suppressed by the dynamics shown above. In the UK, homeownership among 25–34 year-olds fell from around 59% in the early 2000s to 28% in 2025. London's house price to earnings ratio has more than tripled from around 4:1 to nearly 13:1 in a generation.

This is rentier capitalism in its purest form: passive income derived from ownership, not production. Global real estate — valued at $380 trillion (2021, Savills) — is the single largest asset class on earth, dwarfing equities, government debt, and gold combined. Corporate landlords, private equity firms, and speculative investors have turned shelter into a tradeable commodity. The result is a generation locked out of ownership and locked into extractive rents.

"The landlord who happens to own a piece of ground on the outskirts of a great city… watches the busy figures multiplying around him… and all the while sits still and does nothing."
— Winston Churchill, 1909

Source: Harvard JCHS · ONS · Resolution Foundation · Savills Global Research · Shelter

Global South & Imperialism

Extraction
Without Borders

The same logic of extraction that operates within wealthy nations operates between them. When illicit financial flows, debt service, trade mispricing, and unequal exchange are accounted for, the Global South is a net creditor to the Global North — transferring an estimated $2 trillion per year northward. The aid narrative inverts reality: wealth flows from poor countries to rich ones, not the other way around.

The IMF and World Bank enforce structural adjustment programmes that strip developing nations of the very policy tools Western welfare states used to industrialise and build public services. Fifty-four countries are in debt crisis or at high risk. Debt service payments by developing countries reached $443 billion in 2022 — dwarfing the aid they receive — and have risen further since. This is not development. It is extraction with a humanitarian label.

"Europe is literally the creation of the Third World."
— Frantz Fanon, The Wretched of the Earth, 1961

On the IMF and World Bank

Structural adjustment programmes — imposed as conditions for loans — require privatisation of public assets, deregulation of labour markets, removal of trade protections, and cuts to social spending. These are the same policies that built the neoliberal order in the Global North, now exported as non-negotiable conditions to the Global South. A country cannot pursue the Nordic model if the IMF controls its fiscal policy.

The debt itself is often illegitimate — contracted by dictators, inflated by compound interest, or imposed through structural conditions that prevented the growth needed to repay it. Yet it continues to be serviced, year after year, by populations that never consented to it and never benefited from it.

"They no longer need to occupy us. They own the debt."
— Thomas Sankara, OAU Summit, 1987
Ecological Crisis

The Same System.
The Same Crisis.

In 2022, floods submerged a third of Pakistan — a country responsible for less than 1% of historical emissions. In 2021, research published in Nature found the south-eastern Amazon had become a net CO₂ emitter for the first time in recorded history — driven by deforestation and fire. These are not anomalies — they are the predictable output of a system that externalises cost onto nature and the Global South.

Capitalism does not merely pollute — its growth imperative makes ecological destruction structurally inevitable. The same drive to extract maximum value from labour also extracts maximum value from land, water, atmosphere, and biodiversity. These are not separate problems with separate solutions. They are the same logic applied to different commons.

The richest 1% — the same class that captures the majority of economic output — emit more CO₂ than the poorest 50% of humanity combined. The corporations responsible for 71% of global emissions since 1988 are not aberrations: they are the concentrated ownership of fossil capital. Every ton of carbon they burn is extracted value — from the atmosphere, from future generations, and disproportionately from the Global South, which suffers the worst consequences of a crisis it did not cause.

Capitalism cannot resolve this contradiction. The market requires growth; the biosphere requires limits. These are incompatible. Carbon markets, ESG ratings, and voluntary net-zero pledges do not alter this logic — they extend it. The decarbonisation of production requires the same democratic reorganisation that the redistribution of wealth requires. They are the same demand.

"There is no such thing as sustainable capitalism. The system is built on the infinite expansion of production on a finite planet. Eco-socialism is not a branch of environmentalism — it is the recognition that the ecological and social crises share a root."
— Michael Löwy, Ecosocialism: A Radical Alternative to Capitalist Catastrophe, 2015

On Green Capitalism

Carbon trading, ESG investment frameworks, voluntary offsetting, and net-zero pledges share a common assumption: that the market, properly priced, can resolve the ecological crisis capitalism created. This is not a solution — it is a delay tactic dressed in the language of urgency.

Carbon markets commodify the atmosphere and create new financial instruments for speculation. Offsetting allows continued emissions in the Global North in exchange for forestry projects that dispossess Indigenous communities in the Global South. ESG ratings are self-reported and unenforceable. Shell’s net-zero 2050 pledge was accompanied by approved plans to increase oil and gas production through the same period. Volkswagen sold millions of vehicles certified as clean that were engineered to cheat emissions tests. The fossil fuel companies that lobbied against climate legislation for forty years now brand themselves as partners in the green transition — while continuing to expand production.

The ecological commons — air, water, soil, biodiversity — are being treated as they have always been treated under capitalism: as free inputs for extraction and free sinks for waste, until the point of collapse. A socialist reorganisation of production is not an ideological preference. It is a material necessity.

"We have to recognise that climate change is a consequence of the capitalist system. To really tackle climate change, we have to change the system, not the climate."
— Evo Morales, World People's Conference on Climate Change, 2010

On Social Democracy

Social democratic states achieve genuinely better outcomes for their citizens than raw market capitalism does — better healthcare, lower poverty, stronger labour rights. These gains are real and hard-won. But the prosperity that makes them possible rests on a foundation that cannot be universalised.

Western European wealth was built substantially on colonial extraction and is sustained through trade relationships that systematically disadvantage the Global South. The same multilateral institutions — the IMF and World Bank — that protect European capital impose structural adjustment on developing nations, stripping them of the very policy tools used to build Western welfare states. A country cannot pursue the Nordic model if the IMF controls its fiscal policy.

Social democracy is a checkpoint, not a destination — a more humane form of capitalism that has always rested on an exploited periphery. This is the neocolonial order it depends on.

Cuba and China demonstrate that socialist governance — even under decades of economic warfare from capitalist nations — can deliver universal healthcare, eliminate mass illiteracy, and drive development. Cuba, under a six-decade US blockade, developed and exported its own COVID-19 vaccines and deploys medical brigades active in 50+ countries at peak — among the largest international medical deployments globally. China eliminated extreme poverty for 800 million people within living memory, built the world's largest public healthcare expansion, and achieved near-universal literacy in a generation. Their adaptations are not failures of socialism but evidence of its resilience under siege.

"Neocolonialism is the worst form of imperialism. For those who practise it, it means power without responsibility; and for those who suffer from it, it means exploitation without redress."
— Kwame Nkrumah, Neo-Colonialism: The Last Stage of Imperialism, 1965

Policy Demands

A Policy
Programme

Incremental adjustment has not reversed four decades of upward redistribution. These are not untested proposals — each has historical precedent and democratic mandate. These demands form the outline of an eco-socialist programme — grounded in material necessity, democratic accountability, and planetary survival.

01

Democratic Ownership of Production

Worker co-operatives, public ownership of strategic industries, democratic enterprise governance
02

Universal Public Services

Healthcare, housing, education, transport — decommodified and guaranteed as rights, not products
03

Progressive Wealth Taxation

Progressive wealth taxation, inheritance limits, and compulsory acquisition of assets above democratically set wealth ceilings
04

Restoration of Labour Power

Mandatory union recognition, sectoral bargaining, four-day week, living wage indexed to productivity
05

Abolition of Rentier Extraction

Land value taxation, rent control, reform of landlordism — ending the passive extraction of value through mere ownership
06

Planned Ecological Transition

Democratic control of energy, transport, and food systems; public ownership of fossil fuel assets; end all subsidies to extractive industry; just transition guarantees for workers — ecological limits cannot be negotiated with market mechanisms — only democratically owned production can truly subordinate itself to ecological limits.
07

End of Imperialism & Debt Cancellation

Cancel Global South debt, end IMF structural adjustment programmes, pay climate reparations from the Global North, and establish trade terms on Southern terms — not Northern ones
08

Defence of the Ecological Commons

End corporate enclosure of land, water, forests, and atmosphere; criminalise ecocide; return land to Indigenous communities and peasant agriculture; place ecosystem limits above profit — the commons belong to everyone, including those not yet born. The commons cannot be governed by markets. They require collective, democratic ownership.
